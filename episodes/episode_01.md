*One — Jayne*

**The Persephone Lower Docks — A Bar With No Name Worth Remembering — Night**
The bar has no name worth remembering. It sits on the edge of Persephone’s lower docks like a bad tooth — dark, stubborn, and too rotten to pull. The kind of place where the lighting is bad on purpose and nobody asks what you did before you walked through the door.
It is almost midnight. The place is half full of men who look like they’ve made several poor decisions tonight and are currently considering a few more.
In the corner booth, away from the noise and the light, JAYNE COBB sits alone.
He is sixty one years old and built like something that was never meant to move fast but always meant to hurt. His face is a map of bad weather. He wears a battered canvas jacket over a flannel shirt and on his head, pulled low, sits a knitted hat in faded orange — the kind of hat that has no business being on a man of his reputation and yet somehow suits him completely.
There is a glass of whiskey in front of him. There is a cigar burning slow between two thick fingers. And there is a folded piece of paper on the table that he has read four times already.
He picks it up. Reads it a fifth time. Sets it back down.
He takes a long pull on the cigar and blows the smoke toward the ceiling in a slow, unhurried stream. Then he picks up the whiskey and holds it up to the light like he’s checking for something. Whatever he’s checking for, he doesn’t find it. He drinks anyway.
A shadow falls across the table.
Jayne doesn’t look up.
JAYNE
You’re blocking my light.
The shadow belongs to MICK — thirties, wide as a doorframe, with the kind of confidence that comes from never having met anyone who could hit harder than him. He has clearly never met Jayne Cobb. Behind him stands his brother MUCK, who is slightly smaller and considerably less bright, wearing an expression that suggests he is always about three seconds behind every conversation he has ever been part of.
Mick pulls out the chair opposite and sits down without being invited. Muck remains standing, mostly because there is no more chair.
MICK
You Cobb?
JAYNE  (still not looking up)
Depends who’s asking and what they owe me.
MICK
Drayden sent us. Said you were putting a crew together. Said the job was worth our while.
Jayne finally looks up. He takes his time about it. He looks at Mick the way a man looks at a second hand mule he’s considering buying — measuring, unimpressed, already calculating what it’ll cost him when it breaks down.
JAYNE
Drayden talks too much.
He folds the piece of paper and slides it inside his jacket. Then he leans back and takes another pull on the cigar, letting the silence sit there between them like a third person at the table.
MICK
So is there a job or not?
JAYNE
There’s a job.
He reaches into his jacket and produces a small, battered data tablet. He slides it across the table face down. Mick reaches for it.
JAYNE
Don’t flip that over in here.
Mick stops. Pulls his hand back. Looks around the bar instinctively.
JAYNE
Drayden’s people picked up some chatter. There’s a man going after something in that vault tonight. Young. Good with a ship. Been causing enough noise across the Rim that someone put a number on his head worth getting out of my chair for.
He slides the data tablet across. Face down.
JAYNE
He’ll have a crew. He’ll think he’s there for a black box. He won’t be expecting me.
MICK
So we’re not robbing the vault.
JAYNE  (standing, picking up his cigar)
I don’t care what’s in the vault. I care what walks out of it.
He finishes the whiskey. Sets the glass down with the careful precision of a man who has broken too many things in his life to break things carelessly anymore.
MICK
What do you need us for? Word is you work alone.
JAYNE
Word is right. But this vault has four access points and I’ve only got two hands. You cover the north and west corridors, stay out of my way, and don’t touch anything that looks expensive or glows.
He drops a few credits on the table for the drink. Picks up his cigar.
JAYNE
Job’s at midnight. Don’t be late. Don’t be loud. And for the love of all that is decent in this ‘verse —
He looks directly at Muck.
JAYNE
— don’t wear the mirrors.
He walks toward the door without looking back.
Muck looks at Mick.
MUCK
What mirrors?
**Cut to:**

*Two — Saff & Pip*

**A Dockside Bar — Persephone Lower Quarter — Same Night**
The bar is a step up from the one Jayne is sitting in. Only one step. The lighting is marginally better and the glasses are marginally cleaner and that is the full extent of the improvement.
SAFF REYNOLDS (24) sits at a corner table with a man who doesn’t know he’s being assessed. He is thirties, broad shouldered, with the easy manner of someone who has spent a long time being the most capable person in whatever room he’s in. His name is COLE and he is currently explaining something technical to a beautiful woman over drinks which is, as far as he is concerned, an excellent evening.
Saff is watching him the way a cat watches something it hasn’t decided to catch yet. Her drink is untouched. She is wearing her mother’s silk scarf loose around her neck over a tactical vest. Her hands are perfectly still on the table.
She has been steering this conversation for twenty minutes without Cole noticing.
COLE
The thing about military surplus drives is the output tolerance. You can push them past spec if you know the harmonic frequency of the injector coil. Most people don’t know that.
Saff says nothing. She has found that saying nothing makes people keep talking.
COLE
On a variable compression coil you can actually override the governor if you bypass the secondary coupling. Double your output easy. Simple if you know what you’re doing.
At the bar, PIP TAM (24) is eating something that might generously be described as stew. He has the look of someone who is extremely comfortable wherever he happens to be sitting, which is most places. There is engine grease on his left forearm that he hasn’t noticed or doesn’t care about. Probably both.
He has been half listening to the conversation at the corner table for about two minutes — not deliberately, just because wrong information about engines has a frequency he can’t tune out. Like an off-beat in a song you know by heart.
He tries to let it go.
He really does.
COLE
Double your output, easy —
Pip spins around on his barstool with the urgent energy of someone who has just watched a man walk into a wall.
PIP  (genuinely pained)
Oh no no no no —
Cole stops. Looks at the stranger at the bar who is now pointing at him with a spoon.
PIP
Sorry, I’m sorry, I tried to — if you bypass the secondary coupling on a variable coil you don’t double your output. You melt your injector housing. Then you’ve got no output at all. And smoke. Lots of smoke. And then a fire. Definitely a fire.
He does a small explosion gesture with the spoon.
PIP  (shaking his head sorrowfully)
Any engine worth flying deserves better than that.
He turns back to his stew. Then turns back again.
PIP
Sorry. Carry on. It was just really wrong.
Cole stares at him. Looks back at Saff.
Saff is looking at Pip with an expression Cole hasn’t seen on her face once in the last twenty minutes. Like something just became interesting.
Cole opens his mouth.
SAFF  (not looking at him)
Thanks Cole.
Cole closes his mouth. He reads the tone correctly if not the reason. He finishes his drink, sets it down with slightly more force than necessary, and leaves without another word.
The bar settles back into its usual murmur. Saff picks up her drink for the first time. Stands. Walks over to the bar and sits on the stool next to Pip with the easy confidence of someone who has decided something. He doesn’t look up.
SAFF
She?
PIP  (mouth full)
Engines. They’re always a she.
SAFF
How long were you sitting there?
PIP
Long enough. Sorry about your — was that a job interview?
SAFF
Conversation.
PIP
Right. Sorry about your conversation.
SAFF
Don’t be. You did me a favour.
Pip looks at her properly for the first time. Takes her in. Looks back at his stew.
PIP
He really didn’t know what he was talking about.
SAFF
I know.
PIP
You knew?
SAFF
I needed to know if he knew.
Pip considers this. Nods slowly with the expression of someone filing away a useful piece of information about how a particular person operates.
Saff puts a data tablet on the bar between them face down.
PIP
I don’t do Alliance jobs.
SAFF
It’s not an Alliance job.
PIP
Dangerous?
SAFF
Moderately.
PIP
Moderately like slightly dangerous or moderately like fine unless something goes wrong?
SAFF
Moderately like I need someone who knows the difference between a fixed and variable compression coil and doesn’t make fires.
Pip looks at the data tablet. Looks at his stew. Appears to conduct a genuine and thorough internal debate.
PIP
I haven’t finished eating.
SAFF
I’ll wait.
She signals the barman. Pip goes back to his bowl. They sit in easy silence — two people who have only just met and have somehow already worked out how the other one operates.
Pip taps the data tablet once with his spoon without picking it up.
PIP
What’s in the box?
SAFF
Don’t know yet.
PIP
Does it matter?
SAFF
Not to me.
Pip nods. Finishes the last of his stew. Pushes the bowl aside with the solemn ceremony of a man completing something important. Picks up the data tablet.
PIP
My ship needs new compression coils. The real kind.
SAFF
I know a place.
PIP
Course you do.
He flips the tablet. Reads it. His expression doesn’t change dramatically — just a slight brightness in the eyes that could be nerves or excitement. With Pip it’s probably both and probably the same thing.
He sets it down.
PIP
Alright.
SAFF
Alright?
PIP
I said alright. Don’t make it weird.
This time she does smile. Just briefly. Just enough.
**Cut to:**

*Three — Cas*

**Somewhere Above Persephone — The Eleanor — Cockpit — Night**
The Eleanor is not a beautiful ship. She is registered under three different aliases depending on which moon you’re asking from and none of them are her real name, which is painted in small letters under the portside thruster where inspectors rarely look.
She smells like recycled air and instant coffee and the particular tension of people about to do something that requires them to be better than they probably are.
CAS WASHBURNE (25) sits alone in the cockpit. She is lean, slightly too tall for the low ceiling, wearing a Hawaiian shirt under a tactical vest that should be a contradiction but somehow isn’t. Her hair does something complicated that she has stopped arguing with.
The controls move under her hands with the loose ease of someone for whom this is not work but vocabulary. The way some people talk with their hands. Cas talks with a ship.
Persephone fills the lower half of the cockpit window. City lights in the dark. The docks a bright cluster on the western edge.
She doesn’t look at the planet.
She looks at the photograph.
It is taped to the console slightly crooked. A woman in military gear, standing straight, not quite smiling. Nobody on the crew has ever asked about it. Nobody has ever asked because of the specific way Cas looks at it sometimes when he doesn’t know anyone is watching.
She looks at it now.
Then she looks away.
She pulls up the vault schematic one last time on the small cockpit screen. Reads it the way you read something you already know — not for information, just for the feeling of having checked. She folds it away.
CAS  (quietly, to the ship, to nobody, to the photograph)
Alright.
A beat.
CAS
Let’s be good tonight.
The Eleanor drops toward Persephone.
**Cut to:**

*Four — The Briefing*

**The Eleanor — Main Deck — Minutes Later**
The briefing is already underway. Cas stands at the navigation table, schematic up, access points highlighted in blue. Around the table stand three people who have done this before and one who is pretending he has.
DECKER stands to Cas’s left. He is fifties, the kind of fifties that happened outdoors and in poor weather. His face has the specific quality of a man who stopped being surprised by things a long time ago and made his peace with it. He holds his weapon loosely at his side the way some people hold a pen — naturally, without thinking, because it has been in his hand so often it has become part of the grammar of him.
LENA stands opposite. Late twenties. Sharp eyes that move across the schematic the way a reader’s eyes move across a page — quickly, efficiently, filing things away. She says nothing because she is listening.
REX stands at the end of the table. Early twenties. The newest. He is doing an admirable job of looking like a man who belongs in this briefing but his eyes keep checking Cas’s face between sentences to see if he is doing it right.
Cas is explaining the blind spot between columns seven and nine.
CAS
Forty feet of clear approach to the target room. We use it. Lena you’re on the sensors the moment we’re through the door. Rex — you’re on the entrance. Anyone comes through that service door behind us, I want to know about it.
Rex nods. Relieved, if he’s honest. Watching a door he can manage.
She moves her finger across the schematic. Precise. Unhurried.
CAS
We go in, we find the crate, we’re back on the Eleanor inside twelve minutes. Clean.
A beat.
Then, underneath the hum of the ship, almost beneath hearing — a small deliberate click as Decker’s thumb finds the safety on his weapon and moves it in one practised motion. He doesn’t look down when he does it. He is still reading the schematic.
Nobody reacts.
They have all heard that click before. It means Decker is ready. In Cas’s experience Decker is always ready before everyone else in the room and says nothing about it. That is the entirety of why Cas brought him.
DECKER
And if it’s not clean?
Cas looks up. There’s a half beat — not hesitation, just the specific pause of someone who has already run this scenario and knows the answer.
CAS
Then we adapt. That’s what we do.
Decker nods. Once. The schematic gets one more look and then he is done with it. The plan is in his head now. That’s where plans belong.
Rex raises his hand slightly. Stops himself. Raises it again.
REX
What’s in the crate?
Cas folds the schematic away.
CAS
Don’t know.
REX
Does it —
CAS  (before Rex finishes)
No.
Rex closes his mouth. Lena catches his eye across the table and gives him the very small shake of the head that means this is not a new conversation.
Cas looks at the three of them. Decker already thinking about the corridors. Lena already thinking about the sensors. Rex trying very hard to stop thinking about the crate.
He doesn’t say anything else. There isn’t anything else to say. The plan is good. The crew is ready.
He thinks about the photograph on the console.
She goes to get her jacket.
**Cut to:**

*Five — The Job Begins*

**Alliance Sub-Vault — Service Entrance — Night**
The door takes Decker twenty two seconds. He doesn’t mention this.
They move inside in single file. Cas first, then Decker, then Lena pulling the door behind her until it sits closed without clicking. Rex takes his position just inside the entrance, back to the wall, eyes on the door. His job is simple. He’s good at simple.
Cas moves without hurrying. She has the schematic in his head not his hands. He counts doors.
CAS  (quiet, to nobody in particular)
Twelve minutes.
Decker doesn’t respond. He never responds to the clock. He just moves.
**Cut to:**
**Alliance Sub-Vault — South Loading Bay — Same Time**
SAFF comes through the maintenance hatch first. She lands without sound. PIP comes through second and catches his boot on the frame.
PIP  (hissed)
I’m fine.
SAFF
I didn’t say anything.
PIP
You were about to.
She was. She doesn’t confirm this.
The loading bay is large and dark and full of industrial equipment that hasn’t moved in years. Good cover. Good sightlines to the inner corridor. Saff clocks both in approximately one second and is already moving.
Pip looks around the bay with the specific expression of someone who has just walked into a room full of interesting machinery and is physically restraining themselves.
PIP  (whispering)
That relay coupling on the left is older than my dad.
SAFF  (not stopping)
Pip.
PIP
Moving. I’m moving.
**Cut to:**
**Alliance Sub-Vault — North Corridor — Same Time**
JAYNE moves through the corridor like a man who owns it. Unhurried. Shoulders relaxed. One hand loose at his side near Vera’s grip without touching it. He has been in a hundred buildings like this one and they all have the same logic once you stop being afraid of them.
He rounds a corner.
Stops.
MICK and MUCK are inside a cage. It is not a sophisticated cage. It is an old Alliance containment frame — the kind triggered by pressure plates in the floor that nobody has used seriously since the war. A yellow light pulses slowly above them. Mick is standing very still. Muck is not.
MUCK  (loud whisper)
Jayne. Jayne. The floor did something.
Jayne looks at the cage. Looks at the floor. Looks at the mirrors Mick is wearing on his forearms, now reflecting the yellow pulse light in a small cheerful disco pattern across the corridor walls.
He looks at this for one full second.
JAYNE  (quietly, to himself)
Gorramn mirrors.
He steps carefully around the pressure plate and keeps walking.
MUCK  (urgent whisper)
Jayne. Jayne where are you —
He is already gone.
**Cut to:**
**Alliance Command Center — Persephone Station — Same Time**
The room is clean and quiet and lit entirely by screens. There are six of them arranged in a gentle arc, each showing a different feed from inside the vault. Four show empty corridors. One shows static — a feed that went dark twenty minutes ago and hasn’t come back. The sixth shows CAS moving through sub-level two with two crew members behind him.
THE OPERATOR sits in front of this screen. He is forties, neat, with the kind of stillness that comes not from calm but from discipline. He wears an Alliance intelligence uniform without insignia which means his rank is either very low or high enough that insignia would be redundant.
He has been watching the sixth screen for four minutes.
He leans forward slightly. Just an inch. The way a man leans toward something that has become interesting.
An OFFICER stands behind him.
OFFICER
Should I send the response team in, sir?
The Operator doesn’t answer immediately. He watches Cas move through the corridor. Watches her count doors. Watches the way he moves — the economy of it, the confidence.
OPERATOR
Not yet.
OFFICER
Sir the protocol for unauthorised —
OPERATOR  (quiet, not unkind)
I know the protocol.
He picks up a stylus. Makes a note on the tablet beside him without looking away from the screen.
OPERATOR
I want to see what he came for.
On the screen, Cas stops outside a door. Checks the corridor. Nods to Decker.
The Operator watches.
He is very still.
**Cut to:**

*Six — Saff & Pip — The Vault*

**Alliance Sub-Vault — Inner Corridor — Continuous**
The corridor narrows past the loading bay. The walls change here — older, the kind of construction that predates the current Alliance by thirty years at least. Grey composite over something that might once have been stone. The lighting is different too. Yellower. Warmer. Like the building has a memory of when this part of it mattered.
Saff moves along the left wall, checking corners. Pip moves along the right, doing something that looks less like checking corners and more like running his fingertips along the surface of the wall as he goes.
Saff doesn’t comment on this. She has learned in approximately forty minutes of knowing Pip Tam that some things he does have reasons that will become apparent later and some things he does have reasons that will never become apparent at all and there is no reliable way to tell the difference in advance.
They reach the first door.
It is not a modern door. It is a slab of dark metal set into the original stonework, with a locking mechanism on the left side that has no keypad, no scanner, no visible interface of any kind. Just a series of interlocking brass components the size of Pip’s fist, green with age, that sit in a recess in the wall like they have been sitting there since before either of them were born.
Which they have.
Saff reaches for her bypass tool.
Pip’s hand comes out and settles on her forearm without him looking at her. Not grabbing. Just resting there. The universal language for wait.
Saff waits.
Pip crouches in front of the mechanism. He doesn’t touch it yet. He just looks at it the way you look at something you’re trying to hear. His head tilts slightly to the left.
Then he puts his ear against the wall beside it.
SAFF  (low)
What are you —
PIP  (not unkindly)
Shh.
Saff is not a person who gets shushed. She files this away for later.
Pip taps the wall twice with two fingers. Listens. Taps again in a different place. Whatever he hears — and Saff hears nothing but the distant hum of recycled air — seems to satisfy him. He sits back on his heels and looks at the mechanism properly for the first time.
He reaches out and touches the leftmost component. Doesn’t turn it. Just rests his thumb against it and closes his eyes for a second.
Then he does something that makes no sense. He pushes the component inward — not rotating it, not sliding it, just pressing it straight into the wall — holds it there for a count of three, then releases it slowly.
A sound comes from inside the wall. Low. Almost musical. Like something that has been holding its breath for thirty years just exhaled.
The door opens.
Saff stares at it. Looks at Pip. Pip is already standing up and brushing dust off his knee.
SAFF
How did you know that?
PIP  (already moving through the door)
I didn’t.
SAFF
Then what was that?
PIP
She told me. The mechanism. You could feel the tension in it — it wasn’t designed to rotate, it was designed to compress. Someone built it wrong on purpose so people would keep trying to turn it and never push it. It wanted to go inward.
He says this the way other people say the sky is blue. Obvious. Self-evident. Available to anyone who thought to listen.
SAFF
You could feel that.
PIP
Couldn’t you?
Saff looks at the mechanism one more time. Looks at her bypass tool. Puts it away.
She goes through the door.
**Alliance Sub-Vault — Biometric Checkpoint — Minutes Later**
The checkpoint is everything the brass mechanism wasn’t. It is new, clean, and humming with the particular self-satisfaction of expensive technology. A floor-to-ceiling scanner array spanning the full width of the corridor, blue light moving slowly across the passage in a regular sweep. Behind it, the target corridor. Beyond that, the crate.
Saff reads it in three seconds. Military grade biometric. Reads gait, mass distribution, thermal signature. Possibly neural pattern — the newer models do. There is no way through it that she can see.
She looks at Pip.
Pip is already looking at the scanner. Not the way he looked at the brass mechanism — not listening. This is something different. He is very still. His eyes move across the array in a slow, deliberate pattern, left to right, top to bottom, the way you read something you need to understand completely before you touch it.
He takes his small diagnostic tool from his jacket pocket. Not the heavy bypass equipment. The delicate one. The one that looks like it belongs in a medical kit because it does — it was a medical kit before he modified it.
He crouches at the base of the array and starts talking. Very low. Not to Saff.
PIP  (murmuring, almost to himself)
Military grade which means it’s looking for intent as much as identity. Neural pattern scanner on the upper left — that’s the expensive part, that’s what they’re proud of. Thermal’s standard. Gait analysis is the problem, gait’s hard to fool because it’s unconscious, you can change how you walk but you can’t change how you walk when you’re not thinking about it —
Saff watches him. She has seen people talk to themselves under pressure before. It usually means they’re frightened. Pip does not look frightened. He looks like a man reading a very interesting document.
PIP  (still murmuring)
But the neural scanner is reading for hostile intent which means it has a threshold, which means below the threshold it doesn’t flag, which means the question isn’t how to fool it, the question is how to genuinely not intend anything hostile for approximately four seconds —
He sits back. Looks at the scanner. Looks at Saff.
PIP
Do you trust me?
SAFF
I’ve known you for forty minutes.
PIP
Do you trust me right now, in this specific corridor, about this specific problem?
Saff considers the scanner. Considers the corridor behind them. Considers the fact that she has no other options.
SAFF
What do I need to do?
PIP
Walk through it like you’re going home. Not like you’re going to steal something. Like you’ve had a long day and there’s food on the other side and nothing in the world is threatening you.
SAFF
That’s your solution. Think nice thoughts.
PIP
I’ve recalibrated the threshold on the neural scanner down to its minimum sensitivity. You’d have to be actively planning violence for it to flag. But the gait analysis is still running and it will read tension in your body even if your brain is calm. So yes. Genuinely. Think nice thoughts. Walk like you mean it.
Saff looks at the scanner for a long moment.
Then she does something Pip doesn’t expect. Her shoulders drop half an inch. Her jaw unclenches. The thing she carries everywhere — that specific watchfulness, that permanent tactical assessment — goes somewhere else for just a few seconds. She looks, briefly, like someone who is tired and heading home.
She walks through.
The scanner sweeps across her. The blue light moves. Nothing flags.
She stops on the other side and her shoulders come back up immediately, the watchfulness returning like a coat she’d set down briefly and never intended to leave behind.
Pip grins. He can’t help it.
PIP
See? Nice thoughts.
SAFF
Come through the scanner, Pip.
PIP  (already moving, still grinning)
Coming through the scanner.
**Cut to:**

*Seven — Jayne — Solo*

**Alliance Sub-Vault — Storage Level — Night**
The corridor outside the target room is the quietest place in the building.
Jayne doesn’t like it.
He has been in enough buildings like this one to know what silence costs. Empty corridors have a sound to them — the particular texture of air that hasn’t been disturbed in hours. This corridor doesn’t have that texture. This corridor has been disturbed recently. Something moved through here and stopped.
He slows.
Reads it.
Doesn’t like what he reads.
**Cut to:**
**Alliance Command Center — Same Time — Two Seconds**
The feed from the storage level corridor flickers once. Twice.
Goes dark.
The Operator looks at the blank screen for a moment. Then back at Cas’s feed.
He knows that feeling too.
**Cut to:**
**Alliance Sub-Vault — Storage Level — Continuous**
Jayne reaches the door to the target room. He doesn’t go in yet.
He looks down at his arm.
On the sleeve of the coat underneath the canvas jacket, faded almost to nothing, is a patch. Small. Hand-sewn. The kind of thing you put on when you’re young and sentimental and then forget about because you stop being young but never quite stop being sentimental no matter how hard you try.
It says: LEAF ON THE WIND.
He looks at it for one full second.
Then he goes in.

*Eight — The Standoff*

**Alliance Sub-Vault — Target Room — Continuous**
The room is large and dim and smells like old metal and secrets. In the centre, on a reinforced platform, sits a single crate. Black. Unmarked. The kind of crate that doesn’t need a label because anyone who needs to know what’s inside already knows and anyone who doesn’t need to know shouldn’t be in the room.
SAFF and PIP are already there. Saff is reading the crate’s locking mechanism with the focused attention of someone who has approximately ninety seconds before she needs to be somewhere else. Pip is crouched beside the platform looking at something underneath it that has apparently become fascinating.
Jayne takes in the room in one sweep. Two people. Neither of them looking at the shadows. Neither of them expecting company.
He steps into the deepest shadow on the left side of the room and goes completely still.
Sixty four years old and invisible. It took a decade of Mal Reynolds screaming at him to learn this particular skill and he’d never once thanked the man for it. He wasn’t about to start now.
He waits.
The door opens.
CAS comes through first, weapon up, Decker a half step behind him, Lena covering the rear. They take in the room in the same single sweep Jayne did thirty seconds ago. They see the crate. They see Saff and Pip.
Everyone freezes.
Then guns come up on both sides simultaneously — the specific choreography of two groups of professionals who have just discovered they are not alone and have approximately one second to decide what to do about it.
CAS
Step away from the crate.
SAFF
You step away from the crate.
CAS
We had this job first.
SAFF
Define first.
Pip has his hands up. Not because anyone told him to. Just because it seemed like the right thing to do and also he is currently the only unarmed person in the room and would like that fact to be clearly visible to everyone present.
PIP  (helpfully)
I just want to say for the record that I think we can all —
SAFF
Pip.
PIP
Still talking.
SAFF
Stop talking.
Pip stops talking.
The standoff holds. Four people and a crate and the specific vibrating tension of a situation that has not yet decided which direction it wants to fall.
Then a very large shadow detaches itself from the left wall of the room and becomes Jayne Cobb.
JAYNE  (almost pleasantly)
Not today.
Everyone turns. Everyone’s guns follow everyone’s eyes. The geometry of the standoff rearranges itself into something considerably more complicated.
Jayne’s gun is not on the crate.
It is on Cas.
Cas looks at the gun. Looks at the man holding it. Something moves through her face — not fear, nothing as simple as fear — the specific expression of someone doing very fast mental arithmetic and not enjoying the result.
CAS  (quietly, not quite a question)
There’s a bounty.
JAYNE
High one.
CAS
On me.
JAYNE
On you.
CAS
I came here for a black box.
JAYNE
I know.
CAS
I didn’t know about the bounty.
JAYNE
I know that too. Doesn’t change the math.
Cas absorbs this. She does it the way you absorb something you should have seen coming and didn’t — not with drama, not with anger, just with a very quiet internal recalibration that nobody in the room can quite hear but everyone can almost see.
Of course. Should have seen that. How did I not see that.
Saff sees his face.
Something in that specific tired resignation — the way he doesn’t rage against it, doesn’t bargain, just absorbs it and keeps thinking — makes her look at him differently than she was looking at him thirty seconds ago.
She files it away. Doesn’t know what to do with it yet.
Jayne is still looking at Cas. Reading her the way she reads buildings — exits, weights, possibilities. And then his eyes drop.
Just for a second.
To the patch on his arm.
LEAF ON THE WIND.
He looks at it. Looks back up at Cas. At the way he’s standing. At the nervous energy underneath the stillness. At something around the eyes that Jayne has not seen in twenty years and did not expect to see again.
His gun lowers.
Not all the way. Just an inch. Just enough.
He doesn’t know he’s doing it.
**Cut to:**
**Alliance Command Center — Same Time**
The Operator has been watching the standoff on his screen with the expression of a man watching a chess game between three players who don’t know they’re playing chess.
He was enjoying it.
Then the large one lowers his gun.
The Operator’s expression changes. Just slightly. The enjoyment goes somewhere else and something cooler and less pleasant takes its place.
He doesn’t understand why the gun lowered. That is the thing he doesn’t understand. And things he doesn’t understand are things he cannot allow to continue.
He reaches across the desk.
He doesn’t slam it. He’s not a man who slams things. He just presses the button with the same precise economy he brings to everything.
OPERATOR  (quietly)
Send them in.
The officer behind him moves.
On the screen the standoff continues for three more seconds, none of them knowing what just started moving toward them through the corridors.
The Operator watches.
He picks up his stylus. Makes a note.
**Cut to:**

*Nine — The Way Out*

**Alliance Sub-Vault — Target Room — Continuous**
The alarm hits like a physical thing.
Red light floods the room. Somewhere in the walls something heavy starts moving — blast doors, security grids, the building locking itself down from the inside out.
Then boots. Lots of them. Coming fast from two directions.
The standoff evaporates. Nobody discusses it. Guns that were pointing at each other a moment ago are suddenly pointing at the doors and there is an unspoken and entirely pragmatic agreement that the previous situation has been superseded by a more immediate one.
JAYNE
Back corridor. Thirty seconds before they’re on us.
SAFF
The crate —
JAYNE
Leave it.
SAFF
We came here for —
JAYNE
Leave it or stay with it. Your choice. Make it fast.
The first door blows in.
DECKER moves left on pure instinct, drawing fire, creating a gap. It is exactly the right move. It is the move of a man who has done this before and knows what it costs.
He goes down.
No last words. No dramatic pause. Just there and then not there, the way soldiers go when they go, and Cas clocks it in the half second between one action and the next and files it somewhere he won’t be able to look at until this is over.
They move.
Everyone except Pip.
Pip is already back at the crate.
Saff is covering the door, moving, and she looks back and he’s not where he should be and for one specific second she nearly shouts his name.
Then she sees what he’s doing.
He has his diagnostic tool out — the delicate one, the medical one — and his hands are moving across the lock with that same focused Simon-quiet he had at the biometric scanner. Not rushing. Just working. In the middle of a firefight, in a room full of red light and noise, Pip Tam is working.
Eleven seconds.
The crate opens.
He reaches inside and pulls out a black box the size of a hardback book, tucks it under his arm, and appears at Saff’s shoulder.
PIP  (slightly out of breath)
Hacked the lock. Eleven seconds. Personal best.
Saff takes the box from him without a word.
They run.
The back corridor. Running. Cas, Saff, Pip, Jayne, Lena — and Rex, emerging from the service entrance where the first blast door is already closing behind him, sprinting to catch them.
He makes it four steps.
Then he’s not there anymore.
Cas doesn’t look back. She clocks the empty space where Rex should be, the absence of footsteps, and keeps running because stopping is dying and he can feel that particular loss settling into the place where Decker already is and he cannot afford to feel either of them yet.
The corridor branches. Three directions. Two of them loud with boots and red light.
Pip stops.
Not because he’s thought about it. Not tactically. He just stops, head tilted slightly to the left the way it was outside the brass mechanism, and he puts his hand against the wall of the third corridor.
The others are already choosing.
PIP  (quietly, certain)
This way.
Saff looks at him. Looks at the corridor. Looks at the other two options which are getting louder.
She goes.
The others follow because there is no better option and Pip has been right about things all night in ways none of them have fully processed yet.
They run.
Behind them, in the branching corridor, LENA makes the wrong turn.
Or the right turn. Depending on how you look at it.
She doesn’t make a sound.

**Alliance Command Center — Same Time — Two Seconds**

He makes another note.

**Persephone Docks — Loading Bay Seven — Night**
The corridor empties into grey dawn light and the smell of engine fuel and salt air.
And a ship.

JAYNE  (already moving up the ramp)
Move.
Nobody argues.

**The Ship — Cockpit — Continuous**
Cas hits the cockpit at a dead run and drops into the pilot’s chair and her hands are already moving before he’s fully sat down.
The controls are old. Older than anything he’s flown before. Different in the hands — heavier, more deliberate, like flying something that has opinions about how it wants to be handled.
He doesn’t fight it.
He listens.
And the ship — as if she has been waiting for exactly this, for someone who would listen instead of fight — starts to wake up.
Her engine catches. Rough at first. Ragged. Then steadying into something that sounds less like machinery starting and more like someone clearing their throat after a very long silence.
**The Ship — Cargo Bay — Continuous**
SAFF and JAYNE are at the open ramp, returning fire into the hangar as Alliance troops pour through the far entrance. The sound is enormous. The light is strobing red and white.
Saff shoots with the focused economy of someone who learned this the hard way. Jayne shoots with the enthusiasm of someone who genuinely enjoys this particular part of the job and always has.
JAYNE  (roaring toward the cockpit)
Any time, kid!

He still has the black box tucked under one arm.
PIP  (through his teeth)
Come on. Come on come on come on —
He hits the controls with the flat of his free hand. Not in frustration. In the specific way you hit something old that needs to be reminded it still works.
The door groans. Starts to move.
The ship lurches. Rising. The hangar falls away below them, the Alliance troops getting smaller, the firefight becoming briefly abstract and then gone as the ship clears the hangar doors with approximately four feet to spare on each side.
The ramp closes.
Silence.
Relative silence. The engine is loud. But it is a good loud. It is the loud of something working.
**The Ship — Cockpit — Continuous**
Cas has both hands on the controls and her eyes on the sky ahead and Persephone falling away below and the black opening up above and she is thinking about Decker and Rex and Lena and the plan that was good and the things that happened anyway.
He will think about those things later.
Right now he just flies.
And the ship — old, grey, improbable — rises into the black like she was born to it.
Like she never stopped.

**Deep Space — Later**
Stars.
The ship moving through them slowly, unhurried, going nowhere in particular and taking her time about it.

Four people who do not know each other, scattered through her corridors and her rooms, each of them alone with whatever the night has left them.

*Post Credits*

**Alliance Command Center — Night**
The room is quieter now. The screens still glow but the urgency has gone out of them. The Operator sits at his desk in the particular stillness of someone who has finished one thing and is deciding how to begin another.
Spread across his desk: files. Some on tablets. Some on actual paper, printed and stamped and cross-referenced the old way, because some information is too sensitive for a network and some habits die hard.
He has been working through them for twenty minutes.
He picks up one file. Looks at it. Sets it down beside another. Then another. And another.
The files spread across his desk like a constellation taking shape. Faces. Names. Ship registrations. Flight logs. Incident reports going back twenty years. The kind of history that doesn’t show up in official records because it was never supposed to happen.
He finds the large man’s file last.
She looks at the photograph.
Something moves behind his eyes. Not recognition exactly. The edge of recognition. The feeling of a word on the tip of your tongue that you cannot quite reach no matter how hard you try.
He knows that face.
He doesn’t know how he knows it. He doesn’t know from where. But somewhere in twenty years of Alliance intelligence work that face crossed his path and left a mark he didn’t notice at the time.
He sets the file down. Picks it up again. Looks at it longer.
Still nothing. Just the feeling.
He sets it beside the ship registration. Looks at both together.
OPERATOR  (quietly, not quite to himself, not quite a question)
Where do I know you from.
The photograph doesn’t answer.
He picks up his stylus. Makes a note. Underlines something.
He’ll find it. He always finds it.
