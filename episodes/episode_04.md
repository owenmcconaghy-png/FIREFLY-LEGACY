*In which a dead man teaches his son how to fly.*

*One — Morning*

**Deep Space — The Ship — Three Days Out from Rattle Rock — Morning**
The ship moves through the black. Unhurried. No particular heading. Not needing one.
Outside her the stars are the same stars they have always been.
Inside her it is morning.
**The Ship — The Mess Room — Morning**
The coffee is already made when CAS gets there. He doesn’t know who made it. He suspects Jayne, which he would never say out loud, because Jayne would deny it.
He pours a mug. Sits in his usual chair — the one nearest the far wall, the one that faces the room rather than the galley, the one he started sitting in during the first meal they had here and has sat in every meal since without deciding to.
The amber light from the observation windows above moves slow across the table. The ship hums.
JAYNE comes in. Hat on. Plate of something. Sits in his corner. Eats.
Nobody says good morning. They stopped saying good morning somewhere around the third day at Rattle Rock and nobody noticed it happening.
PIP appears in the doorway with engine grease on his left arm already, which means he has been in the engine room for at least an hour, which means he woke before anyone else and went there first because that is what Pip does in the mornings now. He pours coffee. Sits. Picks up a small piece of hardware from his pocket — something from the engine, something he’s been thinking about — and turns it over in his hands the way other people fidget with pens.
SAFF comes in last. She pours coffee without looking at the pot. Sits at the far end. Opens something on her tablet. Not a job. Just something. Her shoulders are down. The watchfulness is still there but it sits differently now — not a defence, just a habit, the way people carry the things that kept them alive long enough to stop needing them quite so urgently.
The four of them sit with their coffee.
Pip sets the piece of hardware down. Picks it up. Sets it down.
Jayne finishes eating. Pushes the plate aside. Looks at the ceiling for a moment the way he does when he is thinking about something he has decided not to say.
Saff turns a page on her tablet.
Cas holds her mug in both hands and looks at the table. At the carved letters. J. COBB. He looks at them the way he looks at the stegosaurus — not reading them, just resting his eyes there the way you rest your eyes on something familiar.
The ship hums.
Nobody needs to say anything.
That is the thing about it. Three weeks ago four strangers stood in this room with their ghosts and their coffee and the silence was the silence of people waiting for something to begin. This silence is different. This is the silence of people who have already begun. Who have been through enough together that the not-talking is its own kind of talking.
A crew.
Not decided. Not announced. Just — arrived at.
Pip picks up the piece of hardware one more time. Looks at it. Puts it back in his pocket.
PIP  (to nobody in particular, meaning the engine)
She sounds good this morning.
Nobody responds. Nobody needs to. They all heard it too.
Cas finishes her coffee. Sets the mug down. Gets up.
Jayne watches him go without asking where.
Pip watches him go without asking where.
Saff turns another page.
The ship hums.

*Two — The Chip*

**The Ship — The Cockpit — Minutes Later**
Cas drops into the pilot’s chair the way she always does now — easy, familiar, the chair fitting differently than it did three weeks ago. Not his shape yet. But closer.
He runs his hands along the console. Not checking anything. Just the way you touch something you’ve come to know.
His fingers find the edge of the display housing. Run along the underside of the console lip. A habit he’s developed without noticing — checking the surfaces the way Pip checks the engine, reading the ship through his hands.
His fingers stop.
There is a small compartment set into the underside of the console. Flush with the housing. No latch visible — you’d only find it by feel, by running your fingers along exactly this edge at exactly this angle.
He presses.
It opens.
Inside: a data chip. Small. Old. The kind that was standard issue a long time ago and isn’t made anymore. It has been here long enough that there is a faint impression of it in the lining of the compartment. Sitting in the same spot for years. Waiting.
Cas holds it up to the light.
Looks at it for a long time.
He looks at the stegosaurus on the console. Looks at the initials H.W. scratched into the housing beside it.
He puts the chip in the reader.
The screen flickers. An old file format — the player takes a moment to find it, the way you find something in a language you almost remember. Then it opens.
The cockpit fills with a voice.

*Three — Log One*

**The Ship — The Cockpit — Continuous — Wash’s Recording**
The recording is slightly grainy. In the background the cockpit looks different. Cleaner. The stegosaurus is already there, taped in exactly the same spot.
WASH sits in the pilot’s chair. He looks younger. More relaxed. He is holding a small plastic pterodactyl and does not appear to notice he is holding it.
WASH  (looking directly at the recorder, completely matter-of-fact)
So. If you’re watching this, one of two things has happened. Either I’m dead — which, let’s not dwell on that, moving on — or I’ve been replaced, which, also let’s not, but statistically more likely given some of the command decisions around here.
WASH
Either way. Hi. I’m Wash. I flew her.
WASH
This is a guide to Serenity. Specifically this is a guide to not dying in Serenity, which sounds like the same thing but isn’t. Any competent pilot can fly a ship. What you’re about to learn is how to fly this ship. There’s a difference and she will absolutely show you the difference if you’re not paying attention.
WASH  (holding up a finger)
First thing. She pulls left on a hard burn if you don’t compensate early. Not at burn initiation — after. About three seconds in she decides she has opinions about your heading and she expresses those opinions through the port thruster. You correct for this by thinking about it before she does.
WASH  (pause)
I know that sounds like I’m describing telepathy.
WASH
I’m describing telepathy.
WASH
Second thing. Her sensors lie in atmo below four thousand feet. Not dramatically. Just enough. Trust your hands over the board down low. The board is optimistic. Your hands know the truth.
WASH  (pause, searching for the word)
Third thing — and this is the important one — she makes a sound before a hard restart that will make you think everything is wrong. A kind of —
WASH
I call it the sigh. Like she’s resigned to something. When you hear that, everything is fine. She’s just clearing her throat. Give her two seconds and she’ll come back. If you panic and override you will make everything that was not wrong very wrong very fast.
WASH  (looking at the recorder for a moment)
There are other things. I’ll get to them.
He is quiet for a few seconds. In the background the cockpit hums. The stars through the glass are different stars. A different part of the verse. A long time ago.
WASH  (quieter now, less performance)
She’s something, this ship.
WASH
I know that sounds like pilot sentiment. It is pilot sentiment. I stand by it completely.
She looks at the stegosaurus. Just for a second. The same one. Right there in the same spot.
WASH  (stopping himself, half a laugh)
Curse your sudden but —
WASH  (to himself, shaking his head)
Wrong dinosaur.
He reaches forward. The recording ends.

*Four — After*

**The Ship — The Cockpit — Continuous**
The screen goes dark.
Cas sits very still.
Outside the cockpit glass the black goes on in every direction.
She looks at the stegosaurus.
She looks at the data chip in the reader.
She doesn’t move for a long time.
Then she gets up.
**The Ship — The Galley — Minutes Later**
She fills a mug. Not because he wants coffee. Her hands just need something to hold.
PIP is at the counter. He has been here the whole time probably, or came back, or never left. It doesn’t matter. He is doing something small and technical with the piece of hardware from his pocket, a focused quiet task that requires just enough attention to justify being somewhere.
He doesn’t look up when Cas comes in.
Cas stands at the counter with her coffee. Looks at nothing in particular.
Pip turns the piece of hardware over. Sets it down. Picks it up again.
Neither of them says anything.
This is not awkward. That is the thing about it. Two people in a galley not talking because there is nothing to say and both of them know it and neither of them needs to fill the space. Pip has the specific quality of someone who understands that sometimes a person needs to stand somewhere for a while and the kindest thing you can do is be nearby without making it a thing.
Cas drinks her coffee.
Pip works on his piece of hardware.
After a while Cas sets her mug down.
Goes back to the cockpit.
Pip watches him go. Looks at the piece of hardware in his hand. Sets it down.
Picks up his own coffee.
**The Ship — The Cockpit — Continuous**
Cas sits back down in the chair.
She looks at the data chip in the reader.
He checks how many logs there are.
Four.
He sits back.
Looks at the stars.
Presses play on log two.

*Five — Log Two*

**The Ship — The Cockpit — Continuous — Wash’s Recording**
The recording is slightly grainier than the first. Different light in the cockpit — something has changed, a fitting replaced, a panel moved. The stegosaurus is still there. So is the pterodactyl, but it’s been moved to a different spot on the console. Wash looks the same. Almost.
Something around his eyes is different. Not older exactly. Just — further in.
WASH  (matter of fact, settling into the chair)
So. Additions to the manual.
WASH
There’s going to come a moment where the board goes dark. Not dim. Not flickering. Dark. Everything off. And I want to be clear that this is different from a systems fault or a power fluctuation or any of the other things that look like catastrophe and aren’t. When I say the board goes dark I mean the board goes dark. No instruments. No readouts. Just you and the helm and whatever you can feel through your hands.
WASH
When that happens — and I’m saying when, not if, because I’ve now had occasion to know the difference — the instinct is to override. To push something. To make something happen because sitting at a dead helm feels like dying and your hands need to move.
WASH
Don’t.
He lets that sit for a moment. Just a beat. Then:
WASH
Here’s what I know. When the board is dark she’s still there. The ship is still there. She’s not going anywhere without you and she knows it. Your job — the only job you have — is to stay at the helm. Not because there’s anything useful you can do. Because someone has to be there when she comes back.
A pause. Longer than his pauses in the first log.
WASH
She will come back. I know that now in a way I didn’t know it before. It’s not mechanical certainty. It’s — she’s stubborn, this ship. She doesn’t go quietly. If she’s still got air in her and someone at the helm she will find a way to come back.
He looks at the console. At the helm. At his own hands on it.
WASH  (quieter)
The other thing. And this is less technical.
WASH
There will be a moment where you are at the helm and something you love very much is on the other side of the ship and you cannot be in both places. And someone will make the call for you about which place you need to be. And you will hate that person for approximately four minutes before you realise they’re right.
WASH  (a beat)
Stay at the helm.
WASH
Whatever is on the other side of the ship — stay at the helm. Because the only way you can help anyone on this ship is by being the person at the helm. That’s the job. That’s all the job is.
He is quiet for a moment. Then he reaches down and taps something on the console. Once. A specific spot.
WASH  (tapping it lightly)
There’s a big red button. Right here.
WASH
You’ll know what it’s for when you need it.
He reaches forward. The recording ends.
**The Ship — The Cockpit — Continuous**
The screen goes dark.
Cas looks at the console.
At the big red button. Right there. The same one. He has flown this ship for weeks and never asked what it was for.
He puts his hand over it. Not pressing it. Just resting there. The way Wash’s hand must have rested there.
He stays like that for a while.
Then he presses play on log three.

*Six — Log Three*

**The Ship — The Cockpit — Continuous — Wash’s Recording**
The recording is different again. Later. The cockpit light is evening light — the specific amber of the ship settling into a long cruise. Wash is in the chair but he’s sitting differently. Less performance. More settled. The pterodactyl is in his hand again but he’s not playing with it. Just holding it.
WASH  (quietly, no preamble)
More additions.
WASH
Silent running. Engines off in atmo, or near a station, or anywhere you need to not be heard. You’ve probably done it. Every pilot has. But I want to talk about what it actually is because most pilots think silent running means making the ship quiet.
WASH  (pause)
It doesn’t. It means making yourself quiet.
WASH
The ship is already quiet. She knows how to be quiet. She’s been quiet in the dark her whole life. What you’re doing when you run silent is learning to be as quiet as she is. To stop adding noise. Your breathing. Your hands on the controls. The little corrections you make because you’re nervous and nervous pilots make corrections and corrections make sound.
WASH  (pause, something passing across his face)
I flew her into a space station once with the engines off. Fully dark. On thrusters only, at about a quarter of what I’d normally use, into an airlock that was —
WASH
It was tight, is what I’m saying. And the whole way in I was thinking about something Zoe told me once about the war. About waiting. She said the hardest thing isn’t the fighting. It’s the waiting before. When there’s nothing to do and everything to lose and your job is just to be still until it’s time.
WASH  (quieter)
Flying Serenity into that airlock I finally understood what she meant.
WASH
The ship was calm. I was calm. Not because nothing was wrong — everything was wrong. But because there was only one thing to do and I was doing it and everything else was noise.
He turns the pterodactyl over in his hand. Looks at it.
WASH  (to the pterodactyl, almost)
I graduated second in my class.
WASH
Took me a long time to understand what that means. Second means you know what first looks like. Means you’ve been close enough to the best to know the shape of it. And when you need to find that place — that still place where the ship and you are the same thing and there’s no noise between you — you’ve been there before. You know the way back.
WASH  (a beat)
That’s what second means.
WASH
Also first place was a fraud but that’s a different story.
The ghost of a smile. Then it settles.
WASH
When it gets worst — and it will get worst — get quiet. Get as quiet as she is. She’ll do the rest.
He sets the pterodactyl down on the console. Carefully. In its spot.
WASH
Log three. End.
He reaches forward. The recording ends.
**The Ship — The Cockpit — Continuous**
The screen goes dark.
Cas sits very still.
Then she gets up.
Not with purpose. Just because his body has been in that chair for a long time and his body has opinions about this that his head is only just catching up with. He stands in the middle of the cockpit and stretches — arms up, back arching, the specific crack of someone who has been very still for hours without noticing. He rolls his neck. Left. Right. Rolls his shoulders.
He runs on the spot for about fifteen seconds. It looks slightly ridiculous in the low light of the cockpit. He doesn’t care. There is nobody watching.
He shakes out his hands. Both of them. The way you shake water off. The way you clear something.
He stands at the back of the cockpit and looks out the glass.
The black. The stars. The ship moving through them. Same as it always is. Same as it always will be.
He breathes.
Once. Twice.
Then he sits back down. Rolls his neck one more time. Looks at the chip in the reader.
CAS  (quietly, to the ship, to nobody)
Alright.
He presses play on log four.

*Seven — Log Four*

**The Ship — The Cockpit — Continuous — Wash’s Recording**
The recording is different in a way that is hard to name.
The cockpit is the same cockpit. The light is the same light. But Wash in the chair looks like a man who has been somewhere and come back and is still deciding what to do with where he’s been. There are no dinosaurs in his hands. His hands are in his lap. He is looking at something beyond the recorder.
He doesn’t open with a joke.
WASH  (quietly)
Last one. I think.
A pause. Long enough that it seems like he might not continue.
WASH
We’re about to do something fairly stupid. On purpose. Which is a sentence I never thought I’d be able to say with a straight face but here we are.
WASH  (looking at the console)
The ship is going to take some of that stupid personally.
WASH
So. Last entry. What to do when she’s hurt. When you’ve pushed her past what she should be able to do and she’s still going — which I’m fairly confident is about to happen in the next little while — you don’t push harder. You don’t back off either. You match her. Whatever she’s giving you, you meet it exactly. No more. No less. You become the thing that’s holding her together from the outside while she holds herself together from the inside.
WASH
When she’s hurt — when you’ve pushed her past what she should be able to do and she’s still going — you don’t push harder. You don’t back off either. You match her. Whatever she’s giving you, you meet it exactly. No more. No less. You become the thing that’s holding her together from the outside while she holds herself together from the inside.
WASH  (pause)
That’s the job at the end. That’s what all of it comes down to.
He is quiet for a long moment. Outside the recorder the cockpit hums. The stars through the glass are different stars again. Another part of the verse. The last part.
WASH
I had a thought, coming through. In the middle of all of it. When it was worst and there was too much happening to think and I just — flew. And something came out of my mouth that I’m not sure I said on purpose.
He pauses.
WASH  (quietly, almost to himself)
I am a leaf on the wind.
A long silence.
WASH
I’ve been thinking about what that means ever since. I don’t entirely know. But I think — I think a leaf doesn’t fight the wind. It doesn’t try to be the wind. It just — goes. It reads every current. It trusts that where it ends up is where it was always going.
WASH  (pause)
I think that’s the whole manual, actually. Everything else I’ve told you is just — practice. For learning to be the leaf.
WASH  (very quietly)
Watch how I soar.
He looks at the recorder for a moment.
Then at the stegosaurus.
Then back.
WASH  (simply)
Fly her well.
He reaches forward. The recording ends.

*Eight — After*

**The Ship — The Cockpit — Continuous**
The screen goes dark.
Cas doesn’t move.
Outside the cockpit glass the black goes on in every direction. The stars. The ship moving through them. The same ship. The same chair. The same stegosaurus taped to the same corner of the same console.
He sits there for a long time.
Then he reaches forward.
He plays log four again.
**The Ship — The Cockpit — Later**
Much later, when the cockpit has cycled through its full range of running light colours and the ship has made all the small sounds she makes when she thinks nobody is listening, CAS is still in the chair.
The chip is in his hand. Not in the reader. Just in his hand.
He turns it over once. Sets it on the console beside the stegosaurus.
He looks at both of them for a moment.
Then he looks at his hands on the controls.
The ship hums.
He puts his hands on the controls properly. Not checking anything. Not going anywhere. Just — present. The way Wash described it. Quiet. Reading the currents.
The ship hums back.
It sounds, very slightly, like an answer.

*Nine — The Note*

**The Ship — Inara’s Shuttle — Later That Evening**
The shuttle is exactly as it always is.
The silk hangings in deep red and gold moving barely in the recycled air. The low table. The mirror with the silver worn at the edges. The warmth that a small space develops when someone has cared for it for a long time.
SAFF sits cross-legged on the bunk. The wooden box is open in front of her. The dried saffron flower beside it.
She is holding the note.
She has been holding it for a while.
She unfolds it.
She reads it.
She reads it again.
Then she folds it carefully along its original creases and places it back under the flower.
She closes the box.
The silk moves in the recycled air.
The ship hums.
— END OF EPISODE FOUR —

*Post Credits*

**The Ship — Inara’s Shuttle — Later**
Saff is still sitting there.
The box closed in front of her. The flower inside it. The note inside it.
She looks at the box for a long moment.
Then she picks it up. Holds it in both hands.
Sets it back down.
She gets up the way she always gets up — shoulders back, already thinking three moves ahead, the watchfulness back in place before she’s fully standing.
Goes to find Cas.

*FADE TO BLACK.*

“Same sky. Different dust. Still flying.”
