*In which the crew meet Dave.*

*One — Rattle Rock Station*

**Rattle Rock Station — Docking Bay — Day**
Rattle Rock Station is exactly what it sounds like.
It clings to the surface of Huang Xing Six’s third moon the way things cling to surfaces out here — not because the location is good but because someone decided to stop here once and then couldn’t afford to leave. The docking bay is carved directly into the rock. Low ceiling. Dim lighting. The smell of recycled air and machine oil and the particular staleness of a place that has been breathing the same atmosphere for twenty years without complaining about it.
The ship settles into her berth with a sound that is half mechanical and half something else — the specific sigh of a vessel that has been flying for four days with a grav boot in declining health and is quietly relieved to stop.
The rattle subsides.
For a moment everything is still.
Then PIP is under the ship before the landing struts have fully extended, diagnostic tool out, already talking to the grav boot with the focused murmuring of a man conducting a very important conversation with something that cannot talk back.
PIP  (murmuring to the engine)
I know. I know. We’re here. You did well.
SAFF comes down the ramp and looks at his boots sticking out from under the hull.
SAFF
How long does she need.
PIP  (from under the ship)
She’s fine now we’re not moving. She just needs the part.
SAFF
Can she fly if we need to leave fast.
A pause. The kind that answers the question before the words do.
PIP
Define fast.
SAFF
Fast like we’re being shot at.
PIP
Probably. Mostly. Don’t push her past point eight and avoid anything that requires a sudden change of vector.
SAFF
That’s nearly everything.
PIP
Then avoid nearly everything.
CAS appears at the top of the ramp. He looks around the docking bay with the professional assessment of a pilot clocking exits. Three of them. Two serviceable. One that probably hasn’t opened since the war.
CAS
Cheerful place.
JAYNE  (already walking toward the far exit)
It’s got a bar.
SAFF
Jayne. We’re not here for the bar.
JAYNE
Dave’s office is above the bar. I wasn’t talking about the bar.
A beat.
JAYNE  (still walking)
The bar’s also good though.

*Two — Dave*

**Rattle Rock Station — Above The Bar — Continuous**
The office is one flight up from the bar and it is a room that takes some time to fully take in.
Not because it is large — it isn’t, the ceiling is low and the walls are raw rock and the whole space has the feel of something that was not so much built as accumulated. But the screens. There are screens on every surface that can hold one, arranged in overlapping arrays that cover the walls from floor to ceiling, each showing a different feed from a different source. News. Traffic patterns. Wave frequencies. Markets. Encrypted channels that flicker with data too fast to read. The combined light of them fills the room with a blue-white glow that makes everyone in it look like they’re underwater.
In the middle of all of it, at a desk that is less a desk and more a flat surface upon which several decades of hardware have been arranged into a working relationship, sits DAVE.
Dave is fifties. Medium height. The kind of face that you look at and immediately forget, which is probably useful in his line of work. He is wearing a knitted sweater that has seen better decades and drinking something hot from a mug that says BEST DAD in large cheerful letters. He does not look up when they come in.
He is reading something.
In the corner of the room, in a chair that has been placed there with some care — angled toward the screens, toward the light, toward Dave — sits VERA.
She is still. Not the stillness of someone waiting. The stillness of someone who is simply present. Her hands are in her lap. She is looking at nothing in particular with the patient quality of a person who has nowhere else to be and has made her peace with that.
She is not a person.
She is very well made. She has been in that chair for an unknown amount of time because Dave likes her there.
JAYNE walks in, clocks the screens, clocks Vera in the corner, and goes completely still.
It lasts one second. Maybe two.
Then:
JAYNE  (to the room, to nobody, to the memory)
Man I knew had one of these.
Dave looks up.
DAVE
Jayne.
JAYNE
Dave.
CAS looks at SAFF. Saff looks at PIP. Pip is looking at the screens with the specific expression of someone trying very hard not to touch anything.
JAYNE  (still looking at Vera)
Beautiful ceremony. She wrote her own vows.
Dave considers this for a moment.
DAVE
She did. I wrote them for her but she delivered them herself. Which counts.
He says this with complete sincerity. Jayne nods once. The subject is closed. They have understood each other perfectly.
Dave looks at the three behind Jayne.
DAVE
These your crew?
JAYNE
More or less.
DAVE
Which is it.
JAYNE
Still working that out.
Dave nods. He stands up and extends a hand to Saff, who is closest, with the manner of a man who has decided that normal social conventions are worth maintaining even in an office carved into a moon.
DAVE
Dave.
SAFF
Saff.
She shakes his hand. Her expression does not flicker. It is only later, when she replays the introduction in her head, that she will have a question about it. She files it away for now.
DAVE  (to Cas, who shakes it)
Cas.
DAVE  (to Pip, who is still looking at the screens)
Pip.
PIP
Sorry. Yes. Sorry. Are those — are those all live feeds?
DAVE
Most of them.
PIP
How many sources?
DAVE
Currently? About four hundred.
Pip looks at the screens. Looks at Dave. Looks back at the screens.
PIP
Right. Yes. Hello. I’m Pip.
Dave almost smiles. He sits back down. Gets straight to it in the manner of a man for whom time is the scarcest resource.
DAVE
Series Three grav boot mount. Original manufacture. You’re lucky — I know a parts dealer on Persephone who has one. Had it for years, waiting for someone to need it. The price is going to hurt.
SAFF
How much.
Dave tells her.
The room is quiet for a moment.
PIP
That’s —
SAFF  (before Pip finishes)
Fine.
PIP
That’s really quite a lot of —
SAFF
Pip.
PIP
Fine. Yes. Fine.
SAFF  (to Dave)
We don’t have that.
DAVE
I know. That’s why I have a job for you first.
He pulls up something on the desk surface. A schematic. A location. A facility on the surface of a moon three systems out — rim world, small, the kind of place that doesn’t appear on most charts because it was never meant to appear on most charts.
DAVE
Alliance research outpost. Minimal staff. Light security by Alliance standards which still means don’t be stupid about it. There’s an item in their primary storage that a client of mine wants very much.
CAS
What kind of item.
DAVE
The valuable kind.
CAS
That’s not an answer.
DAVE  (not unkindly)
It’s the answer you’re getting.
Cas looks at Jayne. Jayne shrugs with the specific economy of a man who has done business this way for forty years and made his peace with it.
DAVE
There’s an Abbey on the same moon. Shepherd community. They’ve been watching that outpost for years — they know the patrol schedules, the shift changes, the two guards who take an extra twenty minutes on their evening break because one of them is teaching the other to play cards. You want that information, you talk to the Shepherds first.
SAFF
You’re sending us to an Abbey for a robbery.
DAVE
I’m sending you to an Abbey for intelligence. What you do with it is your business.
Saff looks at him for a moment.
SAFF
And the part.
DAVE
I’ll have it sourced and waiting by the time you get back. You bring me what I need, you get the part. Clean transaction.
The four of them look at each other. The silent conversation that crews have when they’ve been together long enough to conduct one. Pip’s eyes go from Saff to the screens and back. Cas’s eyes go to the schematic on the desk. Jayne’s eyes don’t move.
Saff makes a decision.
SAFF
Send us the coordinates.

*Three — En Route*

**The Ship — Mess Room — In Transit**
The schematic is spread across the mess table. Real paper — Dave’s preference, apparently, for anything he considers sensitive. The kind of document that doesn’t leave a data trail.
CAS has been studying it for twenty minutes. He has the focused stillness of someone who is not reading a document so much as memorising it.
SAFF is at the far end of the table with a mug of coffee going cold beside her. She is not looking at the schematic. She is looking at the space above it — the middle distance that means she is running through something in her head, testing it, finding its edges.
PIP comes in from the direction of the engine room. He is wiping his hands on a cloth that was clean this morning. He sits down and looks at the schematic upside down from his end of the table.
PIP
She’s holding. For now.
SAFF
How long.
PIP
Long enough. Probably. Don’t ask me to be more specific than probably.
Saff nods. Probably is what they have.
JAYNE appears in the doorway with a plate of something that might be food. He sits in his corner chair without ceremony and starts eating.
CAS
The facility has two entry points. Front entrance — guarded, keycard access, not worth the trouble. Service entrance at the east side — keycard but older model, the kind that’s been on the same code for two years because nobody updated it.
PIP
How do you know they haven’t updated it.
CAS
Because Dave knew they hadn’t. Which means he’s had eyes on this place for a while.
A beat.
SAFF
Which means he knew he was going to need someone to do this job before we walked through his door.
Nobody says anything. This is obvious once said and mildly unsettling once obvious.
JAYNE  (eating)
Dave always has someone for a job. That’s how Dave works.
SAFF
Were we specifically someone or just someone.
JAYNE
Does it change anything.
Saff considers this.
SAFF  (after a moment)
No.
JAYNE  (still eating)
Then stop asking.
Pip looks at the schematic. Turns it around so it’s the right way up for him. Studies the layout with the same methodical attention he gives the engine.
PIP
Primary storage is here. Centre of the building. No windows. Two internal doors between us and it once we’re through the service entrance.
CAS
The Shepherds will know the patrol timing. If Dave’s right about the card game we’ve got a twenty minute window in the evening rotation.
SAFF
We talk to the Shepherds first. We don’t move until we know the window is real.
CAS
Agreed.
She picks up her coffee. Finds it cold. Drinks it anyway.
SAFF
Jayne. The Shepherds. Any history I should know about.
JAYNE
With me?
SAFF
With you.
Jayne sets his fork down. Picks it back up. Sets it down again.
JAYNE
Man I knew had some. With the Shepherds. Not me specifically.
SAFF
What kind of history.
JAYNE
The complicated kind. Old now. They’ll know the ship though.
He picks up his fork again. The subject is, apparently, closed.
Saff files it away. She is getting good at filing things Jayne says away.

*Four — The Abbey*

**Constance Moon — The Abbey of the Penitent Road — Day**
The moon is green. That is the first thing. After weeks of rock and station and the brown-grey palette of frontier infrastructure the green is almost aggressive — low hills covered in something that is almost grass, a sky the colour of pale jade, the particular quality of light that comes through clouds that are never quite gone and never quite there.
The Abbey sits on a rise above a small valley. Stone walls. Old ones — the kind that were built to last and have been doing exactly that for long enough that the distinction between the wall and the hill beneath it has become uncertain. A bell tower. A garden. And at the gate, watching the ship land in the flat ground below with the expression of someone who has been watching things land here for a very long time, a SHEPHERD.
She is old. Not ancient, not frail, but the specific old of someone who has spent a great many years outdoors in all weathers doing things that required both patience and physical capability. Her habit is dark and practical. Her eyes are the kind that don’t miss much and have long since stopped being polite about it.
The crew come up the path from the landing ground. Jayne first, which is unusual. He walks with his hands visible and his shoulders at a particular angle that Saff has started to recognise as his version of non-threatening.
The Shepherd watches them come.
She watches Jayne.
Then she looks past him.
At the ship.
She looks at the ship the way you look at something you recognise from a long time ago and weren’t sure you’d see again. Her expression doesn’t change dramatically. Just something in the set of it. Something settling.
Very quietly. Almost to herself.
SHEPHERD
Serenity.
Not an exclamation. Not a revelation. Just a name.
CAS hears it. Looks at the ship. Looks at the Shepherd. The Shepherd has already moved on, walking toward them with her hands behind her back.
SAFF heard it. She looks at Cas. She gives her the very small headshake that means not now.
PIP heard it. He looks at his own hands. Then at the ship. Then back ahead.
JAYNE heard it. He doesn’t look at the Shepherd. He looks at the ship for just a second — something crossing his face that doesn’t have a name in any language spoken on any moon in the Verse — and then he looks forward and keeps walking.
SHEPHERD  (reaching them, looking at Jayne)
You’ll want Brother Aldric. He’s been watching that outpost since before it was an outpost.
JAYNE
We’re grateful for any help the Abbey can offer.
The Shepherd looks at him for a long moment.
SHEPHERD  (not a question)
You’ve been here before.
JAYNE
Not me personally.
SHEPHERD  (looking at the ship again, then back)
No. Not you.
She turns and walks toward the Abbey gate.
SHEPHERD
Come in. We’ll feed you. Brother Aldric talks better on a full stomach. Most people do.
**The Abbey — The Refectory — Later**
BROTHER ALDRIC is seventies and has the build of someone who spent the first half of his life doing something considerably more physical than prayer. His hands are large and his nose has been broken at least twice and he eats with the focus of a man who treats meals as a practical matter rather than a social one.
He has been talking for twenty minutes. He has not once checked whether they are keeping up. They are keeping up.
ALDRIC
Evening patrol starts at six. Two guards. They do the perimeter first — twenty minutes if they’re prompt, thirty if Sergeant Hollis has been drinking, which on a Seconday he usually has. Then they split. One goes internal. One stays on the east side.
CAS
The east side guard. Is that consistent?
ALDRIC
For three years. The internal guard is the one who plays cards. Private Keel. He teaches a new one every rotation — currently it’s the young woman on comms who is apparently very bad at it and hasn’t worked that out yet. They’re usually in the break room by seven fifteen. Back on post by seven forty at the latest.
Cas looks at Saff. Twenty-five minutes. More than enough.
SAFF
The storage room. Internal security.
ALDRIC
Keypad. Six digit. Changed monthly but they use the same rotation — the last three digits are always the installation date of the outpost. The first three…
He reaches into his habit and produces a folded piece of paper. Sets it on the table. Pip picks it up and reads it. His expression goes very still in the Simon way.
PIP
How do you have this.
ALDRIC  (evenly)
The Alliance uses local contractors for their maintenance. Local contractors talk. We listen.
SAFF
You’ve been planning for someone to go in there.
ALDRIC
We’ve been waiting for someone worth trusting to go in there. Different thing.
He looks at Jayne.
ALDRIC  (quietly)
The man who built this community didn’t believe peace kept itself.
Jayne is looking at the table.
JAYNE
He was a complicated man.
ALDRIC
You knew him.
JAYNE
Knew of him. Mostly.
The Shepherd looks at him for a long moment. Decides something.
ALDRIC
There’s one more thing. The item your broker wants — it’s in a secondary storage case on the left wall. But there’s something else in that room. Primary storage. A black console case about so big.
He indicates with his hands. A box the size of a hardback book.
ALDRIC
We don’t know what’s in it. We know the Alliance has been very careful about it for two years. We know it arrived shortly after a research team came through the system asking questions about signal frequencies. And we know that three people who asked too many questions about it subsequently stopped asking questions about anything.
The table is quiet.
SAFF
You’re telling us this because —
ALDRIC  (a beat, looking at the ship through the window)
I’m telling you this because you’re going to be in that room anyway. What you do with the information is your business.
ALDRIC
We don’t steal. Usually.

*Five — The Job*

**Alliance Research Outpost — Constance Moon — Evening**
Seven fourteen.
The service entrance takes CAS eleven seconds. Not as fast as Decker but close. She doesn’t think about that.
Inside. The corridor is institutional — pale walls, bright lighting, the antiseptic smell of a place that was built to Alliance specification and hasn’t deviated from it since. Everything exactly where the schematic said it would be. The plan is good.
Cas moves left. SAFF moves right. JAYNE takes the centre corridor, slow and deliberate, the way he always moves through buildings — like he owns them, like the building doesn’t know yet that it doesn’t.
PIP stays at the service entrance. His job is the door and the comms and whatever else needs doing that he can reach without moving. He is surprisingly comfortable with this. The door is interesting. Doors are always interesting.
Storage room. Keypad. Pip’s voice in the earpiece:
PIP  (quiet in the earpiece)
Four seven nine — installation date is the last three. Try three one one.
Saff keys it in.
The door opens.
The storage room is small and cool and organised with the particular neatness of an Alliance facility that takes itself seriously. Shelving units. Labelled cases. And on the left wall, exactly where Aldric said it would be, the secondary case. The item Dave wants.
Saff crosses to it. Reads the label. The case is locked with a physical key rather than a keypad — older security than the rest of the room, which is either an oversight or deliberate. She reaches for her bypass tool.
Behind her, CAS has entered. He moves to the right wall. Clocks the primary storage.
The comms crackle.
PIP  (in the earpiece, with the specific tone of someone who has noticed something alarming and is choosing not to panic about it yet)
So. Small problem.
SAFF  (not stopping)
Define small.
PIP
Sergeant Hollis did not have a drink tonight. Apparently. Which means the patrol is running fifteen minutes early. Which means —
SAFF
Where are they.
PIP
East corridor. Two minutes out. Maybe less.
Saff looks at the case. Looks at the bypass tool. The bypass is forty seconds minimum, probably sixty.
She does the arithmetic. The arithmetic is not good.
CAS  (quiet, flat)
Leave it.
She looks at him.
CAS
We don’t have time. We go now.
The plan was good. The variables happened anyway.
Saff puts the bypass tool away.
She looks at the secondary case one more time. Looks at the primary storage on the other wall. Looks at CAS.
He is already moving toward the door.
PIP’s voice in the earpiece: ‘Ninety seconds.’
They move.
JAYNE is already at the junction when they clear the storage room. He has the look of a man who has assessed the corridor and found it acceptable, which is the most reassuring thing about the next thirty seconds.
Sixty feet to the service entrance. The sound of boots from the east corridor — getting louder, not running, just walking, the normal pace of a patrol that doesn’t know it has anything to hurry for.
Forty feet.
Twenty.
PIP holds the service door open. They go through. He pulls it closed behind them — gently, firmly, the specific way you close a door that needs to not be noticed. It clicks shut. The lock re-engages.
Inside, the sound of the patrol passing.
Outside, four people against the wall of an Alliance outpost in the evening light of a green moon, breathing.
Nobody speaks for a moment.
PIP
So. No item.
SAFF
No item.
PIP
No pay.
SAFF
No pay.
A beat.
PIP
No grav boot mount.
SAFF
I’m aware, Pip.
CAS is looking at his hands. The plan was good. Sergeant Hollis chose tonight to stay sober. The plan was good.
He files it in the place where Decker is. Lena. Rex. Things that happened anyway.
JAYNE is looking at something in his hand.
They all look at Jayne.
Jayne is holding a black box the size of a hardback book.
Everyone looks at PIP.
PIP
I — it was just sitting there. On the primary storage shelf. And we were leaving anyway and it looked —
He makes a gesture that attempts to convey that the box looked important in a way that made grabbing it feel like the only reasonable course of action. The gesture is not entirely successful.
PIP  (with complete honesty)
I couldn’t not.
Saff looks at the box. Looks at Pip.
SAFF
That’s not what we came for.
PIP
No.
SAFF
Dave’s not going to want that.
PIP
Probably not.
SAFF
We have no pay and a box we don’t know the contents of.
PIP  (a beat)
Yes.
PIP
Although it’s a very interesting box.
JAYNE is looking at the box.
Not with Pip’s interest. With something more careful. More specific. The look of a man who is putting something together that he is not ready to say out loud yet.
JAYNE  (quietly)
Let’s get back to the ship.

*Six — Back to Dave*

**Rattle Rock Station — Dave’s Office — Later**
Dave takes the news the way a man takes news he has half prepared himself for — without drama, with a very brief closing of the eyes, and then straight back to the practical question of what comes next.
DAVE
The item.
SAFF
Couldn’t get to it. Patrol was early.
DAVE
Hollis.
SAFF
Apparently sober tonight.
DAVE  (with the tone of a man who finds the universe’s timing personally offensive)
First time in eight months.
He is quiet for a moment.
In the corner of the room Vera sits exactly as they left her. Still. Patient. Her hands in her lap.
DAVE
I’ll need to find someone else for the item. That’s — that’s fine. That’s manageable. What I can’t do is pay you for a job you didn’t complete.
SAFF
We know.
DAVE
Which means the part —
SAFF
We know.
The room is quiet.
PIP puts the black box on the desk.
Dave looks at it. Looks at Pip. Looks at the box again.
DAVE
What is that.
PIP
We’re not entirely sure. Alliance primary storage. Looked important.
DAVE
You grabbed something from Alliance primary storage.
PIP
It was right there.
Dave picks it up. Turns it over. Reads what there is to read on the exterior, which is not much. He sets it down.
He looks at it for a long time.
DAVE
I’ll take it against the part. Trade.
SAFF
You don’t know what’s in it.
DAVE  (a beat)
No. But if it was in Alliance primary storage it’s worth something to someone. That’s usually enough.
DAVE
Besides. You need the part. I can see that from here.
He is looking at Pip when he says this. Pip, who has engine grease on his arms and the specific expression of a man who has been worrying about a grav boot mount for four days and is trying not to let it show.
PIP
The mount is — she’s managing. She’ll be fine.
DAVE
How long.
PIP  (firmly)
She’ll be fine.
Dave looks at Saff. Saff gives him the very small nod that means take the deal.
DAVE
The part will be here in two days. You can wait or come back.
SAFF  (before anyone else can suggest otherwise)
We’ll wait.
DAVE
The bar downstairs is —
JAYNE  (already heading for the door)
I know what the bar is.
Dave watches them go. He looks at the box on his desk.
He picks it up again.
Turns it over.
Sets it back down.
In the corner Vera sits as she always sits. Still. Present. Entirely unconcerned with black boxes or grav boot mounts or the particular weight of things that look important without explaining why.
Dave looks at her for a moment.
Then back at the box.

*Seven — Two Days*

**Rattle Rock Station — The Bar — That Night**
The bar is exactly what you’d expect from a bar on the third moon of the sixth planet from Huang Xing. Low light. Hard chairs. A selection of drinks that covers the range from rough to very rough. A barman who has the specific quality of someone who has heard everything and will take whatever you tell him to his grave not out of discretion but out of genuine indifference.
JAYNE is at the bar. He has a glass of something amber and is looking at it the way he was looking at the whiskey in the bar on Persephone. Checking for something. Not finding it. Drinking anyway.
CAS sits down next to him.
They sit in silence for a moment. The companionable kind.
CAS
The Shepherd. She knew the ship.
JAYNE
A lot of people knew that ship.
CAS
She said a name.
Jayne drinks.
CAS
Is that her name.
A long pause. The kind Jayne uses not to buy time but to decide how much of the truth this moment has earned.
JAYNE
Yeah.
CAS
Serenity.
JAYNE
Yeah.
Cas looks at her drink. She doesn’t have a drink. She looks at the bar instead.
CAS
Who flew her before.
Jayne is quiet for a long time.
JAYNE  (quietly)
Good man. Best I ever saw in a cockpit. Better than he knew.
Cas absorbs this. Looks at the bar. Looks at Jayne.
CAS
What happened to him.
Jayne picks up his glass. Looks at it. Sets it down without drinking.
JAYNE
That’s a longer conversation than tonight.
Cas nods. She has learned that with Jayne the door closing is still a door — it’ll open again when it’s ready.
CAS
Alright.
Jayne picks up his glass. Drinks.
JAYNE  (after a moment, looking straight ahead)
He’d have liked you.
Cas doesn’t say anything. There isn’t anything to say.
The bar goes on around them. Rattle Rock Station doing what it always does, which is carry on regardless, indifferent to ghosts.
**Rattle Rock Station — The Ship — Two Days Later**
The part arrives in a crate that is slightly too large for it, packed in material that suggests whoever sourced it knew it was irreplaceable and treated it accordingly.
PIP opens the crate in the docking bay. He lifts the mount out carefully. Holds it up to the light. Reads the serial number. Reads it again.
His expression doesn’t change.
The tell.
He takes it into the engine room and is not seen for four hours.
On the second day Saff goes to check the stores.
They are full.
Real food. Sealed protein that doesn’t smell like the reconstituted kind. Three canisters of coffee that are better than anything Dave’s bar had on offer. Fuel cells at capacity.
Nobody asked for any of it.
She stands in the cargo bay for a moment looking at it.
Then she goes back to what she was doing.
**Rattle Rock Station — The Ship — Day Two — Later**
When he emerges he is covered in a new biographical layer of grease and is carrying the old mount, cracked clean through the housing. He holds it up for the others to see.
PIP
Four more days and she’d have gone completely. We wouldn’t have made it to the next system.
He says this with no drama. Just fact. A man reading a diagnostic result.
SAFF
But she’s good now.
PIP  (a beat, looking at the engine room door)
She’s good now.
PIP
Better than good. She’s happy.
He says this the way he says everything about the engine. Not as a metaphor. Just as fact.
JAYNE is already running the pre-flight checks. Not because anyone asked him to. Just because they need doing and he knows how.
CAS
Where are we headed.
Saff is leaning against the cargo bay wall with her arms crossed and the particular expression she gets when she has been thinking about something for two days and has arrived at a conclusion.
SAFF
Aldric said three people stopped asking questions about that box. Which means someone in the Alliance knows it’s gone by now.
CAS
And Dave has it.
SAFF
